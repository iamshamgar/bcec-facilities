Week 1: Get Your Bearings Days 1-5

Know what you have, know what's broken right now, and know who to call.

Days 1-2: The Critical Document Hunt

Find these documents. They may be in a filing cabinet, on someone's computer, in a church office, or they may not exist. Knowing which ones are missing is just as important.

  • Building keys and access codes — get a full set. Were the previous director's keys collected?
  • The vendor/contractor list — who does the church call when HVAC breaks, toilet won't stop, roof leaks? This is gold. If it doesn't exist, building it is your top Week 2 priority.
  • Insurance certificate (property insurance) — what's covered, when does it renew?
  • Most recent fire inspection report — were there violations? Were they corrected?
  • Any existing maintenance logs or work order history — even a notebook or spreadsheet
  • The facilities budget — what was budgeted this year? What's been spent? If no one can produce this, that's a finding in itself.
  • Certificates of occupancy, building permits for recent work, elevator inspection certificates (if applicable), backflow preventer test reports, fire alarm monitoring contracts
If you can only find one thing, find the vendor list. When something breaks at 10pm on Saturday before a Sunday service, you need phone numbers.

Days 2-3: The Building Walk-Through

Walk every room, hallway, closet, the roof (if safe), parking lot, mechanical rooms. Bring your phone for photos and notes.

Life Safety Items (fix immediately if found)

  • Exit signs — are they lit? Do they point the right direction?
  • Emergency lighting — press the test button on battery-backup lights. Do they come on?
  • Fire extinguishers — present, visible, current inspection tags? (Annual inspection; tag has a date and punch mark.)
  • Tripping hazards — torn carpet, uneven thresholds, cracked sidewalks
  • Blocked exits — doors chained shut, storage in front of exits. THIS IS A CODE VIOLATION. Fix today, not tomorrow.
  • Handrails — loose? Missing where there are stairs?
  • Visible mold, standing water, or active leaks
  • Electrical panels — accessible (no storage within 3 feet)? Breakers labeled?

Building Condition Items (note but don't panic)

  • Roof — visible ponding water, damaged flashing, missing shingles?
  • Walls and ceilings — water stains (past or active leaks), cracks, peeling paint
  • Restrooms — leaking fixtures, running toilets, proper ventilation
  • HVAC — is the building comfortable? Thermostats working? Unusual noises?
  • Parking lot — potholes, faded striping, ADA-compliant accessible spaces with proper signage?
  • Exterior — drainage (water should flow away from foundation), sidewalk condition, exterior lighting

Mechanical / Utility Rooms

  • HVAC equipment — note brand and model numbers, photo the nameplates
  • Water heater(s) — photo nameplates
  • Electrical panels — main and sub-panels. Labeled?
  • Fire alarm panel — display says "Normal"? Anything else needs attention.
  • Sprinkler system (if present) — find main riser and control valves. Valves open? Test/inspection tag present?
Take photos of everything.

Days 3-5: The People Interviews

Church administrator or office manager
  • What facilities problems do people complain about most?
  • Who has been handling maintenance requests? How do they come in?
  • Any upcoming events that need special setup or have facilities implications?
  • What's the building schedule — who uses the building and when?
  • Is there a facilities budget I can see?
Custodian / janitor (if there is one)
  • What's broken right now that you've been working around?
  • What do you wish someone would fix?
  • Where do you get your supplies? Is there a budget for that?
  • Are there areas of the building that worry you?
This person is your most valuable source of ground truth.
Long-time volunteer who helps with facilities ("the building guy")
  • What's the history of this building? Known problem areas?
  • What major repairs in the last few years?
  • What have you been wanting to do but haven't had budget/approval for?
  • Who are the good contractors you've worked with?
Every church has one. Find them. Buy them coffee.
Senior pastor or executive leadership
  • What are your expectations for me in this interim role?
  • What are the top three facilities issues from your perspective?
  • Are there any capital projects planned or being discussed?
  • What's the approval process for spending — what can I authorize, what needs sign-off?
This conversation sets your scope.

Vendor Relationships

For each key vendor, make contact:

  • Introduce yourself as the new interim facilities director
  • Confirm account is in good standing
  • Ask about outstanding proposals, open work orders, or overdue services
  • Ask about contract terms — what's covered, response time, renewal date

Key vendors to identify/verify

  • HVAC contractor
  • Plumber
  • Electrician
  • Fire alarm / sprinkler company
  • Locksmith
  • Pest control
  • Landscaping / snow removal
  • Janitorial supply vendor
  • General handyman or general contractor
  • Roofing contractor
  • Elevator company (if applicable)

Budget Reality Check

  • What's the annual facilities/maintenance budget?
  • What's been spent year-to-date?
  • How does this year compare to last year?
  • Is there a separate capital budget for big projects?
  • Are there reserves or sinking funds for major future expenses?
Benchmark: Churches should spend about $2-4 per square foot per year on maintenance and operations (not including utilities). If the building is 20,000 sq ft and the budget is $10,000/year, that's $0.50/sq ft — the building is being starved.

Week 4: Synthesize and Plan Days 22-30

Write a "State of the Building" Summary

One document. 2-3 pages. For leadership. Include:

  1. Overall building condition — honest assessment
  2. Compliance status — are inspections current? Which need scheduling?
  3. Top 5 priorities — ranked by safety-first hierarchy, with cost estimates
  4. Budget assessment — is current budget adequate? What's the gap?
  5. Vendor status — key relationships in place? Gaps?
  6. Staffing reality — what does it take to run this building? Staff vs. volunteers vs. contractors? Sustainable?

Establish an Operating Rhythm

  • Set up a way to receive maintenance requests (shared email, Google Form — "tell Larry who tells someone" is not a system)
  • Weekly walk-through — same day, same route, every week
  • Monthly budget check — what was spent, what's coming up
  • Seasonal prep checklist — what needs to happen before summer (AC), winter (heating, pipes), Easter (deep cleaning)?

Set Expectations with Leadership

Have the direct conversation:

  • "Here's what I've found."
  • "Here's what needs attention immediately."
  • "Here's what needs budget."
  • "Here's what a permanent facilities director should be focused on."
  • "Here's what this role actually requires — for the hiring conversation."

That last point connects to your hiring challenge. After 30 days in the role, you'll have real data on what the job demands, making the compensation conversation concrete.

Key Principles to Remember Always
Don't do the work yourself.

Identify, prioritize, manage. Use contractors and volunteers for execution.

Water is the enemy of buildings.

Any water where it shouldn't be gets priority.

Deferred maintenance is debt with interest.

A $500 fix ignored becomes $5,000. Frame it as stewardship.

Document everything.

Photos, dates, costs, decisions. The permanent director will need this handoff.

Life safety doesn't wait for budget approval.

Fix it now, explain later. No one was ever fired for unchaining a fire exit.
